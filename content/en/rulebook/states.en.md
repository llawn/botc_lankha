In Blood on the Clocktower, players can be lots of things

* this character or that one, *drunk* or *sober*, *alive* or *dead*, *healthy* or *poisoned*.

Of course, we don’t mean literally if **Lankha** is drunk or **Paul** appetites isn't satisfied or **Melho** looks dead.

These are called **states**.

Basically, they’re permanent elements of a player that are independent from each other.
For example, if you’re *drunk* and change your character, you stay *drunk*. If you’re *poisoned*, you can be *drunk* too.

##  Life and Death

At any given time, a player is either *alive* or *dead*.
Generally, players die during the day from execution and die at night from characters’ abilities.

### Execution is different from death.

Sometimes, a player may be executed but remain alive rather than die.
Players may be executed multiple times, and even dead players may be executed again, just to be sure.
Regardless of whether the group executes an alive or dead player, this counts as the one execution allowed for the day.

### A dead player cannot die again.

If a dead player is attacked by the [Demon](/en/characters/demons/imp), for example,
they do not die again, and the group does not learn that they died last night.

##  Alignment and Character

At any given time, a player is either *good* or *evil*.
Characters start as either good or evil, but their alignment may change.
Sometimes, a [Townsfolk](/en/characters/townsfolk/) will be evil.
Sometimes, a [Demon](/en/characters/demons/) will be good.

If a *good* player has an *evil* character, or an *evil* player has a *good* character,
turn their character token upside-down to remind yourself that the player’s alignment is the opposite of the color of the token.

### Character is independent of alignment.

If a player changes alignment, their character stays the same, and vice versa.

{{< example >}}
If the [Goon](/en/characters/outsiders/goon) becomes evil, they’re still the Goon.
Or if the [Pit-Hag](/en/characters/minions/pit-hag) turns the good [Juggler](/en/characters/townsfolk/juggler)
into the [Witch](/en/characters/minions/witch), then the Witch is still *good*.
{{< /example >}}

### The player learns of changes...

If a player’s alignment or character changes, they learn this at the earliest opportunity, in secret.
You will usually show the **YOU ARE** info token and then their new character or alignment.

The night sheet or character almanac will tell you when an alignment or character changes,
prompting you to wake the changed player and inform them.
Learning a new character or alignment isn’t information in the normal sense.
It is not affected by *drunkenness* or *poisoning*, or by *characters* such as the [Vortox](/en/characters/demons/vortox).
This is so that players know their own alignment and character.

### ...with a few exceptions.

Sometimes, a player thinks they are a character or alignment that is different from their true character or alignment.
For example, the [Drunk](/en/characters/outsiders/drunk) and the [Lunatic](/en/characters/outsiders/lunatic) are designed to not know who they really are.

##  Drunkenness and Poisoning

At any given time, a player is either *sober* or *drunk*, and either *poisoned* or *healthy*.
Being *drunk* and being *poisoned* do the same thing.

Alive and dead players alike can be *drunk* or *poisoned* or both.

### A drunk or poisoned player has no ability.

A *drunk* [Slayer](/en/characters/townsfolk/slayer) cannot slay anybody,
a *poisoned* [Demon](/en/characters/demons/imp) cannot kill anyone,
a *drunk* [Virgin](/en/characters/townsfolk/virgin) cannot cause an execution with their ability,
a poisoned [Courtier](/en/characters/townsfolk/courtier) cannot make anyone drunk.

If a player tries to use their *“once per game”* ability while *drunk* or *poisoned*,
it doesn't work and they do not get to use it again. It is gone.

### They can get their ability back.

If a *drunk* player becomes *sober* again, or if a *poisoned* player becomes *healthy* again, they regain their ability.
That said, if they used their *“once per game”* ability already, then tough luck.

### Do not tell them they are drunk or poisoned!

Instead, act just like they’re *sober* and *healthy*.
{{< example >}}
A drunk [Monk](/en/characters/townsfolk/monk) still wakes each night and chooses a player to protect... but that player won’t be *protected*.
A *poisoned* [Demon](/en/characters/demons/imp) still wakes to attack a player, but nobody dies,
and the Demon won’t know exactly why.
{{< /example >}}

Sometimes you may want to hint to them that they are *drunk* or *poisoned*,
such as by showing the [Undertaker](/en/characters/townsfolk/undertaker) that the
[Mayor](/en/characters/townsfolk/mayor) got executed when nobody is claiming to be the [Mayor](/en/characters/townsfolk/mayor),
but such times are exceedingly rare.

### You can give them false information.

A *drunk* or *poisoned* player does not have an ability, but they think they do.
If their ability gives them information, you can give them incorrect information.
{{< example >}}
A *drunk* [Empath](/en/characters/townsfolk/empath) still wakes each night and gets shown a finger signal,
but you can show the wrong number of fingers.

A *poisoned* [Undertaker](/en/characters/townsfolk/undertaker) gets shown the character card of the player who died by execution today,
but you can show the wrong character card.
{{< /example >}}

You’re not required to give incorrect info, you can give true information or mixed them. But you can—and you usually should!

### Drunkenness and poisoning do not cancel out.

A *poisoned* *drunk* does not become *sober* or *healthy*! They’re just both *poisoned* and *drunk*.

### Abilities used on a drunk or poisoned player work normally.

{{< example >}}
An [Empath](/en/characters/townsfolk/empath) correctly learns the alignment of their *drunk* neighbors.
A [Fortune Teller](/en/characters/townsfolk/fortune-teller) correctly identifies a poisoned Demon.
{{< /example >}}

If the [Duchess](/en/characters/fabled/duchess) - a [Fabled character](/en/characters/fabled) that grants information to 3 players - is visited by a *poisoned* [Slayer](/en/characters/townsfolk/slayer), then the [Slayer](/en/characters/townsfolk/slayer) gains correct information.
In this case, the [Duchess](/en/characters/fabled/duchess)’s ability is working properly, while the [Slayer](/en/characters/townsfolk/slayer) has no ability.

## Always give correct information about the rules

Even to *drunk* or *poisoned* players. They need to be able to trust you at least that much.

### The player is drunk or poisoned, not the character

Even though it is common to refer to a *drunk* or *poisoned* character.
If a *drunk* player becomes a new character, they are still *drunk*.
If a *poisoned* player swaps characters with another player, the player remains *poisoned*.

### The timing of drunkenness and poisoning can vary slightly with unusual character combinations

Normally, if an ability is a permanent ability or is already affecting the game,
the player loses their ability when they become drunk or poisoned,
and that ability resumes when they become *sober* and healthy again.

- The [Tea Lady](/en/characters/townsfolk/tea-lady) is *poisoned*, so she does not *protect* her neighbors.
  Later, the [Tea Lady](/en/characters/townsfolk/tea-lady) becomes healthy, so she protects her neighbors again.


- The sober [Witch](/en/characters/minions/witch) has *cursed* a player.
  The [Witch](/en/characters/minions/witch) becomes *drunk*, so that player is not *cursed*.
  Later, the [Witch](/en/characters/minions/witch) becomes *sober* again, so that player is *cursed* again.

If an ability is triggered or used when the player is *drunk* or *poisoned*, the ability is wasted.
It has no effect now, and no effect later on, even if the player later becomes *sober* and *healthy*.

- A *poisoned* [Sweetheart](/en/characters/outsiders/sweetheart) dies.
  The [Sweetheart](/en/characters/outsiders/sweetheart) does not make a player *drunk*,
  even if the [Sweetheart](/en/characters/outsiders/sweetheard) becomes healthy later on, because she died.

- A *drunk* [Innkeeper](/en/characters/townsfolk/innkeeper) chooses two players to *protect*.
  They are not *protected*, even if the [Innkeeper](/en/characters/townsfolk/innkeeper) becomes *sober* later on.

If an ability is triggered or used when the player is *sober* and *healthy*,
the ability works normally, even if the ability relies on some condition being true in the past
and the player was *drunk* or *poisoned* at that time in the past.

- A *sober* [Juggler](/en/characters/townsfolk/juggler) wakes at night and learns correct information,
  even though the [Juggler](/en/characters/townsfolk/juggler) was *drunk* when they made their guesses earlier that day.

Clocktower is designed so that such odd timing situations are rare.
When in doubt, refer to the character’s almanac entry for clarification.

### TIPS

To run *drunkenness* and *poisoning* well, listen to the bluffs of the evil players,
and support those bluffs wherever possible.

{{< example >}}
If the *drunk* [Ravenkeeper](/en/characters/townsfolk/ravenkeeper) chooses to learn about player 1,
who is the [Baron](/en/characters/minions/baron) but bluffing as the [Mayor](/en/characters/townsfolk/mayor),
you can help the *evil* team if you show the Ravenkeeper the Mayor card.
If the drunk Ravenkeeper instead chooses to learn about a *good* player,
it can be helpful to show an *evil* character card instead.
{{< /example >}}

Normally, you should make *drunkenness* and *poisoning* as harmful as possible to the *drunk* or *poisoned* player.

##  Madness

**Madness** is introduced in the [Sects & Violets](/en/scenarios/sects-and-violets/) edition.

It’s fairly rare, with only about a dozen characters that cause madness over the many editions.
It is more like a real-world state than a game-state.

You cannot look in [the Grimoire](/en/toolbox/) and see which characters are *mad* or not,
but you can watch and listen to what people are saying to determine if players are *mad* or not.

### When a player is mad about something

Being mad about something means they’re trying to convince the group that something is true.
Some players are instructed to be *mad* about something, and some players are instructed to not be *mad* about something.

### When a player is instructed to be mad about something, but they aren’t, the Storyteller might give them a penalty.

Likewise, if they are *mad* about it, the Storyteller might give them a bonus.

The specific instructions, benefits, and penalties of madness are written on the character sheet.

---

- The [Mutant](/en/characters/outsiders/mutant)’s ability is
  *“If you are ‘mad’ about being an Outsider, you might be executed.”*

  In other words: "don't convince people you are an Outsider"

  If the [Mutant](/en/characters/outsiders/mutant) player,
  is trying to convince the group that he is the [Mutant](/en/characters/outsiders/mutant),
  or another [Outsider](/en/characters/outsiders) character by saying:

  - *I am not a Townsfolk... but I’m not going to tell you my character.*

  The Storyteller is welcome to *execute* him: if he is not a *Townsfolk* he should be an *Outsider*.
  Unless you are playing with other *good* categories (*No people lies to be a minion or demons unless end of game*).

  But less strictly he shouldn't lied to be another Outsider character or tell he is the [Mutant](/en/characters/outsiders/mutant).

  He might also say something like:
  - *I am not going to tell you who I am, but I’m definitely not the Mutant*.

    with a mischievous grin on his face

  or he may even say nothing at all while other players accuse him of being the Mutant.
  In either case, the Storyteller may judge that he is trying to convince the group that he is the Mutant.
  In the first case, he is saying one thing and meaning another.
  In the second case, he is saying nothing at all but hoping that, because of it, the group will conclude that he is the Mutant.
  In either case, the Storyteller might execute him.

- If the Storyteller doesn’t spot the Mutant telling anyone that he is an Outsider,
  but an evil player comes up to the Storyteller in private and says, “He told me he is the Mutant,”
  then that’s evidence enough that he’s trying to convince the group that he is the Mutant,
  so the Storyteller can execute him.

- The [Cerenovus](/en/characters/minions/cerenovus)’ ability tells a player that they should be mad about being a specific character or else they might be executed.
  The [Cerenovus](/en/characters/minions/cerenovus) chooses player 2 to be mad about being the [Sage](/en/characters/townsfolk/sage),
  so player 2 should do her best to convince the group that she is the [Sage](/en/characters/townsfolk/sage),
  or else she risks being executed.

  If she simply sits there and says nothing, so she has certainly acted the way a Sage would act—staying quiet—but
  she has not tried to convince the group that she was in fact the Sage.

  Something much more convincing would be required to convince the *Storyteller*, so she can be executed.

### Players are never forced to be mad.

Players may say whatever they want at any time—they are never compelled to say anything they do not wish to say.
With madness, however, they are incentivized to say particular things and disincentivized to say others.

If a player flat out says, “I am mad,” or otherwise heavily implies it, then the Storyteller can give them the appropriate penalty or remove the appropriate benefit.
This kind of statement is usually a player’s way of saying “I do not wish to be mad about this thing, and I would rather take the penalty.” Even if a player merely implies that they are mad, that counts.

### A previously mad player can talk about it without penalty.

Once they are no longer *mad*, players can let the group know that they were mad without suffering the consequences
{{< example >}}
A player that was picked by the [Cerenovus](/en/characters/minions/cerenovus) two nights ago,
but not last night, can happily tell the group this without fear of execution.
{{< /example >}}

A *dead* [Mutant](/en/characters/outsiders/mutant) no longer has their ability, so need not fear its consequences.

### The Storyteller, are the final judge about who is and who is not behaving madly.

There are no rules about what must or must not be said. What matters is what you think the player is trying to do.

Generally, be firm in your judgment.
If players come to believe that all they need to do is put in a half-assed effort to avoid the madness penalty, then that’s what they will do.
IT’s not the end of the world if this happens, but the fun in being mad is in being incentivised
to spread lies and misinformation to your own team and to not be able to own up to it until you are sane again!

### Advanced players should know how to play madness well

Take this into consideration when deciding whether or not to penalize a player for madness.

{{< example >}}
An advanced player instructed to be mad about being the [Flowergirl](/en/characters/townsfolk/flowergirl)
had better be telling the group which days the Demon voted and did not vote.
If he puts in a solid effort convincing the group he actually is the Flowergirl, then he lives!
{{< /example >}}

**Beginner players** should be given a little more leeway.
As long as you believe they are putting in a genuine effort to convince the group, then you can be more forgiving.
Players don’t have to actually convince anyone in order to count as mad... they just need to put in a sincere-looking effort.

### Working with text messages with madness characters

If the game master decides to use **madness**, they must disable the ability to send messages to other players.
If the game master really wants to use it, they must set it up as a Discord channel so that the narrator can read all conversations.
Or the game master is confident about his players.
