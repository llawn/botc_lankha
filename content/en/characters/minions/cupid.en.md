##  Summary
"Each night, choose two players: they become lovers. If one dies, the other dies too."

Cupid's arrows bind two souls together—for better or worse, until death do them part.
* Each night, Cupid wakes and chooses two living players. These players become lovers.
* If one lover dies (by any means), the other lover dies immediately.
* Lovers may be changed each night—Cupid's affections are fickle.
* A lover who dies because of their partner's death does not trigger the Hunter's ability.
* Cupid may choose themselves as one of the lovers.

##  How to Run

Each night, wake Cupid. They point to two players (they may point to themselves). Mark both players with the **LOVER** reminder. Remove any previous **LOVER** reminders. If a lover dies, the other lover dies immediately. If Cupid dies, the current lovers remain lovers.

##  Examples

				Cupid makes Marie and Pierre lovers on night one. Pierre is executed. Marie dies too.

				Cupid makes themselves and Claire lovers. Claire is killed by the Werewolf. Cupid dies. The Werewolf now has one fewer Minion.

##  Tips & Tricks

* Make lovers of two good players to reduce the village's numbers when one dies.
* Make yourself a lover with a powerful good player to create doubt—they won't know if you're good or evil.
* Change your lovers each night to keep the village guessing. If the same two players are lovers every night, they may figure out Cupid's pattern.
* Pair the Werewolf with a good player as a lover to protect the Werewolf—if the Werewolf is targeted, their good lover also dies, creating confusion.

##  Bluffing as Cupid

Bluffing as Cupid is risky—you need to explain who you paired each night. If you claim two players were lovers and they don't die together, your bluff is exposed. Use it cautiously, and only pair players who are likely to die soon.
